The implementation of water policies and water resources management require relevant tools of hydrologic engineering. In the case of urban floods, which have strong socio-economic impacts, the design of water structures (hydraulic spillways of stormwater, dams, etc) necessarily involves the analysis of sub-daily extreme rainfall series. It is the main purpose of Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) rainfall curves which aim at estimating rainfall return levels at different time scales. In West Africa, IDF curves are all the more important as the region has faced since the last two decades a concomitant increase of flood hazards, demographic rates, rural exodus and urbanization. However, due to observational or practical limitations, IDF curves are rare in West African countries and the methodologies used to build them often suffer from scientific weaknesses. The main purpose of this study is to estimate IDF curves in Senegal by applying a parsimonious but reliable statistical framework based on both extreme value and fractal theories. The study is based on long sub-daily rain-gauge series collected by the National Weather Service of Senegal (ANACIM). The distribution of intensities is described by a GEV distribution, while a simple scaling factor is used to link the different time scales. Beyond the fact that robust IDF curves are now available for Senegal, the results show interesting features as a clear North-South gradient in the parameters of the GEV distribution (a classical feature of the West-African monsoon) while the scaling parameter is affected by the distance to the ocean.

After a period of severe drought in the 1970s and 1980s, the Sahel region of West Africa has witnessed a relative increase in precipitation. It has also been struck by several floods of unprecedented magnitude, in spite of annual rainfall totals remaining below those of previous rainy periods. This study aims to attribute the increased risk of flooding to either land use or precipitation regime changes. Notably, this study seeks to apply rigorous statistical methods developed for the attribution problem in climate science to the domain of hydrology. Selected methods include fraction of attributable risk (FAR) and optimal fingerprinting. Stochastic rainfall scenarios are generated based on the rainfall climatology of the recent past and are fed into a hydrological model representing surface properties associated with land use changes. The presentation will be focused on how the rainfall simulator is adapted to reflect pertinent findings, such as the evolution of extreme events. Precipitation trends and internal variability are analyzed using the AMMA-CATCH Niger data set (30 stations) and data from national daily rain-gauge networks. The Dargol basin, located on the right bank of the Niger river, is used as a test case. The proposed methodology for the attribution problem can be adapted for application at other sites.
